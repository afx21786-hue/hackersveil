Part One Before the World Broke The ones we love become our whole vocabulary.
Chapter One The Only Real Thing in the World
"The most dangerous thing a man can do is love someone so completely
that without them, he no longer recognises himself in the mirror."

He had lived inside machines his whole life. Not the steel-and-oil kind — the invisible kind, the ones humming quietly beneath the skin of the modern world: servers, networks, firewalls, encrypted channels. Dominic Voss had understood code before he could fully understand people, had mastered the language of ones and zeros long before he could master the language of his own heart. By twenty-two, he was already a ghost in the machine — unseen, untraceable, brilliant in the way that a scalpel is brilliant: cold, sharp, designed to cut deep. The people who hired him never knew his face. They only knew his handle: Erebus. Named, fittingly, after the ancient god of darkness.

By day he moved through the city like everyone else — coffee, crowds, the indifferent hum of a Tuesday. He wore plain clothes and kept his eyes down and gave nothing away. Nobody looked twice at Dom. That was exactly how he wanted it.

But night was his. Night was when Erebus woke up.

His apartment was a cathedral of screens. Four monitors arranged in a semicircle above a battlestation of a desk — keyboards, drives, a tangle of black cables he knew by memory, the way a pianist knows the exact weight of every key. The amber glow of the monitors was the only light he needed. He had once gone four days without opening the curtains and hadn't noticed. The digital world asked nothing of him except precision. It didn't want him to feel. It didn't want him to be warm. And for a long time, Dom had believed that was enough.

Then came the rain. Then came the Tuesday that cracked his whole architecture in half.

He had chosen the café — Caruso's, a narrow, dimly-lit place that smelled of old wood and cardamom — because the wifi was untapped and the corner table had a clean sightline to both exits. He had been running surveillance data on a particularly filthy government contractor, three screens mirrored onto his laptop, his coffee growing cold beside him. He was not the kind of man who let his coffee grow cold. He was not the kind of man who let anything distract him.

He heard her before he saw her. Laughter — bright and uncontained, like something that had been bottled and finally let loose. Then a sudden collision of bodies and table, and his coffee was a warm brown ocean spreading across his keyboard and his lap and every instinct he had screamed the wrong thing entirely, because instead of moving to save the hardware, he looked up.

She had both hands clapped over her mouth. Her eyes — dark and enormous, full of a terrible suppressed hilarity — were already apologising even as the rest of her face was clearly deciding whether to burst out laughing. She had an orange scarf looped around her neck, raindrops still glittering in her hair like she'd run through a storm and not minded at all. She was nineteen years old, and she was the most alive thing Dominic Voss had ever seen in his life.

"Oh no," she said. Then she lowered her hands. The laughter won. "I'm so sorry — your laptop — oh no —"

"It's fine." He hadn't meant to say it like that. Gently. The word had simply come out gentle, as if his body had made a decision his brain had not been consulted on.

She grabbed a fistful of napkins from the dispenser and pressed them to the table, blotting furiously, still laughing, and it was the most chaotic, unself-conscious thing he'd watched a person do in years. Everyone else in the café was staring. She didn't notice, or she didn't care — either way it was extraordinary.

"I'm Mari," she said, still blotting. "Short for Mariela. I bump into things a lot, you should know this upfront, it's basically a personality trait at this point."

"Dom."

She looked up then — properly looked at him — and something in her expression shifted. The laughter settled into something softer. "You look like you haven't smiled in a long time," she said. Not cruelly. Just honestly. "That's a shame. You've got a good face for smiling."

He did not know what to say to that. He had spent years learning to say exactly the right thing in exactly the right sequence to crack open a firewall, to talk his way into a private channel, to execute a flawless social engineering attack on a Fortune 500 company. But a nineteen-year-old girl with rain in her hair and coffee on her hands had just made him completely speechless.

She looked at his ruined screen, then at his face, and her grin returned — irrepressible, unstoppable, like the tide. "Looks like I just hacked your drink, stranger."

Dom Voss looked at her across the wreckage of his coffee and forgot — just for a second, just one terrifying, luminous second — how to breathe.

* * *

He walked her home that evening under a single umbrella, the rain coming down in silver sheets. She talked the entire way. She talked about her sister's terrible taste in men and the novel she was reading and how she believed that cities had personalities and that this city — their city — was fundamentally a melancholic city, "but the good kind of melancholy, you know? The kind that makes jazz make sense." She asked him what he did. He said he worked in data. She accepted this completely, asked no follow-up questions, and moved on to a story about a street cat she'd been feeding for six months that had just shown up one morning with kittens.

He said almost nothing the entire walk. But when they reached her door — a blue-painted door on a narrow street where the streetlamp threw amber circles on the wet pavement — she turned to him and said, "You know what you are, Dom? You're one of those people who listens like they mean it. That's rarer than you think."

He thought about that for three days.

He thought about it while running surveillance traces at two in the morning. He thought about it while he cracked a firewall protecting the private servers of a ministry he had no love for. He thought about her laughter, the orange scarf, the way she had said his name like it was already familiar.

On the third day he went back to Caruso's.

She was already there.

* * *

Their first real date — because that afternoon at Caruso's was the second one, though neither of them said so — was a Friday evening when the air tasted of coming autumn. Mari had told him, quite seriously, over two cups of coffee, that she had a place she needed to take him. "Non-negotiable," she said. "Don't ask questions. Just wear something that doesn't need to stay clean."

She took him to the Varga Amusement Park — a place that should have been dead twenty years ago, that by rights should have been rubble and redevelopment, but which somehow still turned its lights on every evening with the stubborn vitality of something that refuses to be finished. The rides were old. The paint was peeling on the carousel horses. The whole park had the slightly threadbare glamour of a ballgown worn too many times, and Dom thought it was one of the most beautiful places he'd ever seen.

They rode the Ferris wheel at midnight.

At the top, with the city spread below them like a circuit board — all lit squares and dark channels and the bright arterial lines of the main roads — the wheel stopped. Maintenance, or fate. It didn't matter. They hung there above the world in a swaying gondola while the wind moved Mari's hair across her face and the city hummed its insomnia below them.

She turned to look at him. Not the laughing girl from the café now — something quieter, more serious, more real. A face he hadn't seen yet and already couldn't imagine not knowing.

"You know what I love about you, Dom?" she said. Her voice was low, almost lost under the wind. "You see the whole world as ones and zeros. As a system to be understood. To be cracked open." She paused. "But when you look at me — I can tell. When you look at me, you don't see a system. You see something you can't calculate. And I think that scares you." A small smile. "And I think that's the only code I ever want to live inside."

He kissed her then — and it was the first honest thing he had done in years. Soft at first, tentative, the way you approach something fragile and astonishing. Then something in him stopped calculating and just fell, and the kiss deepened, and the gondola swayed, and the city below them blazed on, indifferent and eternal, and for the first time in as long as Dom could remember, the world outside his skull ceased to exist entirely.

"He had cracked governments. He had walked invisible through the most fortified systems in the country. But she was the first thing in his life that he hadn't tried to understand — and the first thing he hadn't needed to."

What followed was fourteen months that Dominic Voss would spend the rest of his life returning to, pressing his fingers into like a bruise that proves something once existed.

They built a world. It had the dimensions of two apartments and a handful of favourite streets and that small round table at Caruso's and the park in October and the balcony above the city, and it was the most real geography Dom had ever inhabited.

She learned his hours — the strange inverted schedule of a man who worked when the city slept — and rather than fight it she adapted, showing up at eleven at night with food she'd cooked, sliding in beside him at his desk with her legs tucked beneath her, reading while he worked, the warmth of her a constant quiet miracle at his elbow. He would look up sometimes and just watch her — head bent over a book, lower lip tucked between her teeth, completely absorbed — and feel something in his chest that he had no technical vocabulary for.

It was a Tuesday afternoon in November — he remembered because November Tuesdays had been the particular shade of grey that made the apartment feel like the only warm place in the world. He was showing her how a vending machine in the university corridor had a firmware vulnerability so basic it was almost charming. The hallway was empty, fluorescent lights buzzing overhead, and she leaned into him as he talked her through the keystrokes, her chin almost on his shoulder, her breath warm against his neck.

"Type this," he murmured. "Exactly this."

She typed. She got it wrong twice, cursed, giggled, tried again. On the third attempt the machine gave a mechanical shudder of compliance and dropped two chocolate bars. Mari let out a shriek of pure delight that echoed off every surface in the corridor, and before he could react she had spun around and thrown her arms around his neck and kissed him — fierce and joyful and tasting of the coffee she'd had that morning — and they nearly fell into the machine together.

"We are unstoppable," she announced breathlessly against his mouth. "A criminal empire. Starting with chocolate bars and moving up."

"You typed it wrong twice."

"Details." She kissed him again. "Details."

They fought, of course. The way people fight when they're close enough to hurt each other and brave enough to try. There was the night she wanted the stupid romantic film and he had a job deadline and she stole his laptop with all the dignity of someone executing a heist, tucked it under the bed, and sat on the edge of the mattress with her arms folded and an expression of absolute conviction.

"The job will still be there," she said. "The deadline can move. But I am here, right now, in this present moment, and I choose to spend it watching people fall in love over a series of elaborate misunderstandings. You are coming with me."

"Mari—"

"Dom."

The thing was, she was right. Not just about the film — about the larger principle she was always gently, persistently trying to teach him: that the present moment is the only one we actually own. He always knew it intellectually. She made him feel it in his bones.

They ended up on the living room floor, the film forgotten, laughing at something she'd said about the terrible CGI in the opening credits, her head on his chest, his arm around her, the room warm and full of the particular happiness that has no name but that every person who has ever been truly loved knows on sight.

"Love isn't a program, Dom," she said later, soft and sleepy, tracing the line of his jaw with one finger in the dark. "You know that, right? You can't debug it. You can't back it up. You can't encrypt it against loss." A pause. "But it's also the only thing in the world that hackers can't delete. Even if they try. Even if they have to."

He held her tighter. He didn't answer. He had no words adequate to that particular truth.

* * *

Their best hours were the late ones. After midnight, when the city had finally quieted to a murmur, they would take blankets out to the narrow balcony — barely wide enough for two people and a bottle of wine — and lie together while the lights of the city below them pulsed like something breathing. She would talk about her dreams. She wanted to travel — not tourist travel, real travel, the kind where you stay long enough in a place to understand what the light looks like there at different hours, to learn which bakery the locals actually went to, to sit in parks and simply be part of a place. Portugal, she said. Japan. The mountains of Georgia. "I want to see it all," she said once. "Every inch. And I want you there beside me for every inch of it."

And Dom, who had been nowhere — who had seen the entire planet through the cold glass of other people's surveillance systems, who had walked through foreign cities in satellite footage and drone feeds but never felt foreign rain — Dom lay in the dark with this girl against his chest and felt the specific ache of someone who has just been given a reason to want a future.

He told her things he had told no one. About the jobs. About the clients — government contractors, oligarchs, shadowy corporations with names that meant nothing and purposes that meant everything terrible. He told her about the firewalls he'd cracked and the data he'd found and what some of it had cost him in sleep. She listened to all of it — listened with the careful, complete attention she gave everything — and she never flinched and she never judged him and she held him a little tighter.

"You're not a bad person doing bad things," she said once. "You're a good person trapped in a system that makes bad things the only available option. There's a difference. And someday you'll figure out how to change the system instead of just moving through it."

He thought about that for weeks. He still thought about it.

Some nights their love was slow and reverent — quiet and unhurried, the way you move when you're trying to memorise something by touch. Other nights it was urgent and fierce, torn loose from somewhere deep in both of them, as if they both knew, somewhere beneath the joy of it all, that happiness like this has a price, and the invoice always arrives.

But he pushed that thought away. He was good at pushing things away.

He was good at a lot of things that would eventually fail to save him.

"For the first time in twenty-two years of living inside machines, Dominic Voss felt warm. Not the warmth of proximity or habit — but the deep, cellular warmth of being known. Completely, dangerously, irreversibly known."
Chapter Two The Vanishing
"Grief begins not in the grave but in the last ordinary moment —
the moment just before the world changes its mind about you."

It was an ordinary Thursday. That detail would haunt him more than anything else — the sheer, casual ordinariness of it. Not a night with a red sky or a stillness in the air that might have served as warning. Just a Thursday in March, the city going about its grey and unremarkable business, pigeons on the railings and a woman selling flowers on the corner and the smell of rain that hadn't arrived yet.

Dom had been at his desk since noon, deep in a commission — tracing dark money through seventeen shell companies across four continents. He was in that particular tunnel-state of focus where hours passed like minutes, where the world outside the screens ceased to matter, where he was pure intent and no body at all.

At 7:42 p.m. his phone lit up on the desk beside the keyboard. He saw her name and felt the small, reliable warmth that her name always produced in him. He glanced at the message without breaking his rhythm.

On my way home, love you more than Wi-Fi and chocolate combined ❤️
Don't forget to eat something real, hacker boy.

He smiled at the screen — the quick, private smile she reliably produced in him, the one that felt like a different person's smile, softer than anything else he ever wore — and went back to work.

At nine o'clock he realised she wasn't there.

At nine-fifteen he told himself she had probably stopped somewhere. A friend. A shop. The bookstore on Vidal Street that she could never walk past without going in.

At nine forty-five he called. It rang out. He called again. He texted. Three grey ticks. Delivered. Not read.

By ten o'clock his hands were already moving toward the keyboard with a different purpose.

He told himself it was nothing. He told himself this with the focused, deliberate quality of a man who already knows that it is something, who is simply buying himself a few more minutes inside the last ordinary moment before the world changes its mind about him forever.

* * *

He did not sleep that night. He did not sleep the night after. He became, in the space of seventy-two hours, something he barely recognised — a man assembled entirely from panic and precision, all the warmth of the past fourteen months driven underground by the cold terrible engine of fear.

He hacked city surveillance with a savagery he'd never brought to a personal task before. Traffic cameras, transit logs, ATM feeds, the grainy footage from the convenience stores and pharmacies and parking garages of every street between Caruso's café and her apartment. He tracked her route like a man following blood through snow. He found her on six cameras in the first eight blocks. Walking fast, earphones in, shoulders slightly hunched against the cold. The orange scarf around her neck.

After the seventh camera, she was gone.

Not obscured. Not around a corner. Not stepped into a shop. Just — absent. As if the world had simply closed around her like water.

He traced her phone to its last signal. The industrial zone on the south side of the river — a neighbourhood of warehouses and empty loading bays and the kind of darkness that doesn't come from absence of light but from absence of eyes. The ping was brief. Then nothing. As if the signal had not been lost but surgically removed.

* * *

Those three days had a particular texture that would never leave him. The apartment — their apartment, the one that held every evidence of her — became a kind of torture chamber made of love. Her red scarf hung on the back of the chair and he couldn't bring himself to move it. Her half-eaten bag of strawberry candies sat on the kitchen counter and he caught himself standing in front of it at three in the morning, just looking at it, trying to process the idea that she had been alive and reaching into that bag not four days ago.

Her perfume was still on his pillow. This was perhaps the worst thing. He would lie on the bed — not to sleep, sleep was impossible, but to be horizontal for a while, to give his body the rest his mind refused — and the smell of her would be there, the specific combination of jasmine and something warm and organic that was simply her, and his throat would close completely and a sound would come out of him that he didn't know he was capable of making.

He kept returning to a Sunday morning three months ago. She had been asleep when he woke up — deeply, contentedly asleep, her hair across his shoulder, her breathing slow and even. He had lain very still, not wanting to wake her, watching the early light change on the ceiling. He had thought: this is it. Not in a grand way — quietly. Simply. This is the thing I was missing without knowing I was missing it. This specific warmth. This specific breath. This specific person.

He had not told her that. He wished, with a fury that had no exit, that he had told her that.

He played her voice messages on loop — there were eleven of them saved, ranging from practical instructions about buying milk to a two-minute recording of her reading him a poem she'd found that she thought he'd like, her voice warm and slightly self-conscious, pausing before certain lines to say "wait, this part, listen." He played them until the tenth time, when something in him broke open completely and he threw the phone across the room with a scream that tore his throat raw.

He picked the phone up. He played them again.

* * *

On the fourth night — 2:17 a.m., his eyes burning, three days of stubble and two of showering — a message appeared on his primary monitor. Not an email. Not a chat notification. It was simply there, in plain text, as if someone had walked into his digital space without knocking and written on his wall.

Warehouse district. Dock 17. Black van. No plates.

He stared at it for four seconds. He checked every trace he knew — sender, routing, infiltration method. Nothing. Whoever had sent it had done so from somewhere that simply did not exist in any accessible record. A ghost message, from a ghost sender, pointing toward the place where his ghost girl had vanished.

The text deleted itself. Cleanly. As if it had never been.

Dom was already reaching for his jacket.

* * *

Dock 17 smelled of salt water and rust and something metallic he didn't want to name. The van was gone. But it had been there — he could read the scene the way a programmer reads a crash log, all the residual data of something catastrophic. Tyre marks — deep, from a heavy vehicle that had stopped fast. Broken glass, safety glass, the kind from a car window struck from outside. And then, caught on the edge of a rusted bolt like a red flag on a battlefield:

A torn piece of orange fabric.

Her scarf.

Stained dark at the edges. Dried, already.

Dom knelt on the cold concrete and held the fabric in both hands and did not think anything at all for a very long time. The pain was beyond thought — below thought, in some older part of him that predated language. It was the pain of something being ripped out by the root. He pressed the fabric against his mouth and made a sound that had no name in any language, a sound that was grief in its most animal form, and the empty dock absorbed it, and the water moved darkly below the pier, and the city offered no comfort at all.

He did not know — could not have known — that seventy metres away, in a black car with tinted windows, a seventy-one-year-old man in a crisp dark suit sat watching him through a monitor feed, his face very still, his jaw very tight, and said nothing at all. Just watched his grandson come apart at the seams and noted, quietly, that it was time to begin.

Chapter Three The Silent Guardian
"A father's love is patient. A grandfather's love is merciless —
it will burn down whatever needs burning to protect what matters."

Zir Voss was, to those who dealt with him professionally, an exceptionally unremarkable man. This was not an accident. He had constructed unremarkability with the same meticulous care that other men constructed power — because he had long understood that in the real world, the two things are not opposites but synonyms. The most powerful things in the world are invisible. Gravity. Radiation. Money. The systems that actually govern.

He was seventy-one years old. He had silver hair, cut short, and the kind of posture that certain classes of military man develop: perfectly erect, not from vanity but from the muscle memory of decades. His hands were large and still bore the thickened knuckles of a man who had, in his youth, resolved problems with those hands. His eyes were grey and very calm — the eyes of someone who has looked at terrible things for so long that nothing surprises them anymore.

His suits were always black. He wore no jewellery. He spoke quietly and made you lean forward to hear him, which meant he was always the person the room was leaning toward.

To the outside world — to tax records and company registrations and the handful of journalists who had tried, briefly and ill-advisedly, to investigate him — Zir Voss was a shipping magnate. Comfortable. Legitimate. The kind of successful man whose success is so complete it becomes boring, and boredom is the best camouflage in the world.

What he actually was, was the seventh leader of Silido.

"Silido was not a criminal organisation — not in the way people imagine criminal organisations, with their chaos and their petty violence and their need for external validation. Silido was, in the oldest sense, a state. It simply chose not to be visible."

They had been operating for sixty years. In that time they had made and unmade three governments, orchestrated the removal of two presidents without a single body being traceable to them, and quietly buried the careers — and occasionally the breathing — of every official who had threatened to expose what the country's real machinery looked like under its democratic hood. They were not motivated by ideology. They were motivated by order — specifically, their order, the order they had decided was preferable to any alternative.

They had a code. It was unwritten, unarguable, and absolute. Blood is blood. Whatever the cost. Whatever the method. You do not let family fall.

Zir had known about Mari's abduction within hours of it happening. He had known because he had men in enough places that almost nothing happened in this city without a ripple reaching him. He had known the name of the man behind it — Hades, which was a pseudonym that told you everything you needed to know about a person's self-image — and he had known the destination, and he had known what that destination meant for the girl who was taken there.

He could have ended it in forty-eight hours. He had people who could have walked onto that island before Mari had been there three days and brought her back. He had the capability. He had the men.

But he had also watched his grandson for twenty-two years. He knew Dom's particular brand of integrity — the stubborn, self-destructive refusal to acknowledge the family he came from, the carefully maintained fiction that he was, in all the ways that mattered, self-made, clean-handed, alone. Dom knew, in the abstract, what his grandfather was. He had chosen not to look at it. Zir had, for years, respected that choice.

He had decided, watching Dom fall apart at Dock 17, that respect had its limits.

He would not simply extract the girl and hand her back, eliminating Dom's agency in his own story. That was not what Dom needed. What Dom needed was to become the person capable of handling the truth of what the world was — the person capable, eventually, of inheriting the responsibility Zir had been carrying alone for thirty years. The crisis was cruel. The crisis was also, Zir had concluded with the pragmatism of a man who had seen a great deal of terrible things, an opportunity.

He would give his grandson breadcrumbs. He would clear the path. He would, when the time came, provide the army.

And Dom would become what he was always meant to become.

In his control room — a clean, climate-controlled space beneath his private estate that contained more processing power than most national intelligence services — Zir assembled his inner circle. Twelve men and two women who had served him with absolute loyalty for a combined total of over two hundred years. He looked at their faces across the long dark table and said what needed to be said.

"My grandson's girl has been taken by Hades." He paused. Not for effect. For precision. "Dom will hunt him. We will not let him hunt alone — but he will not know we are there. We clear the path. We eliminate the obstacles he cannot see. We feed him what he needs to find the right doors." He let the silence settle. "And when the moment comes that he cannot win alone, we arrive."

No one questioned this. No one ever questioned Zir's directives — not because they were afraid to, but because in sixty years of making decisions, he had not yet been wrong in any way that could not be corrected.

"Blood is blood," said the man at his right hand — Tobias, sixty, scarred, loyal as stone.

"Blood is blood," the room answered.

Zir looked one last time at the monitor showing Dom kneeling at the edge of Dock 17, still holding the piece of red scarf, and said nothing. But his jaw moved once, tightly, and anyone who had known him long enough would have recognised, in that smallest of movements, the only form of grief Zir Voss permitted himself.

Part Two The Descent What love becomes when it has nowhere left to go.
Chapter Four Descent into Hell
"Rage, unchecked, is madness. But rage with a target —
rage with a name carved into every bullet —
that is something else entirely. That is purpose."

He became a different person in the weeks that followed. It was not a dramatic transformation — not a sudden shedding of self, not a single night where he looked in the mirror and decided to become someone else. It was more like watching steel cool. The man who had loved Mari Delgado with his whole bruised heart was still in there — he could feel him, the way you feel a limb that is asleep, all pins and needles and useless pressure. But on top of that man, something harder was forming. Something without edges that could be exploited. Something without warmth that could be leveraged.

Dom Voss was building himself into a weapon. He did not do this consciously. He did it the way the body builds scar tissue — automatically, urgently, because the alternative was to remain an open wound, and an open wound cannot hunt.

He trained every morning before dawn. He had always been physically capable — the wiry, controlled strength of someone who manages stress through movement — but now he trained with a focused brutality that alarmed the men at the boxing gym who trained alongside him. He went past endurance into something else, something that looked from the outside like punishment. He came home with split knuckles and bruised ribs and didn't notice either. His body was simply the delivery system for the thing he was becoming.

At night he hacked.

Not the careful, methodical work of a professional — not the elegant intrusions he had spent years perfecting, built on patience and misdirection and surgical precision. This was something rawer. He tore through firewalls like they offended him personally. He went places no single operator had gone before, pulling data from government servers and ministry private channels and the dark corners of infrastructure that was never meant to be accessed by anyone with a conscience. He worked eighteen hours at a stretch, sustaining himself on bad coffee and the burning, constant pressure of finding the next door, the next name, the next breadcrumb that would tell him where she was.

The anonymous messages continued. Every few days — always different routing, always untraceable, always arriving with surgical timing when he had hit a wall. A set of coordinates. A decrypted file. A name, sometimes, with a brief accompanying data package that told him exactly who that name belonged to and why it mattered. He should have found this more alarming. He didn't have the bandwidth for alarming. He had only the bandwidth for forward.

He followed the chain for weeks. Each link led to the next. Officials who had flown in private planes to coordinates in international waters. Financial transfers between government accounts and shell companies registered in jurisdictions that made things invisible. The same private island appearing again and again — listed on official records as Eden Holdings Ltd., a private resort. No reviews online. No public footprint. Just money flowing in, and silence flowing back out.

"Every name he found, he carved into a private file he kept encrypted on a drive that never touched the internet. Not a list of targets — he told himself it was not a list of targets. It was a record of the truth. The truth that would demand to be answered."

He would think of her, sometimes, in the middle of a night hack — involuntarily, the memory arriving like an uninvited hand on his shoulder. Her voice. The specific quality of her laugh — the way it always built, starting slow, then accelerating, then completely surrendering to itself. He would be mid-keystroke, data streaming across his screens, and suddenly he would be back on the balcony, her head on his chest, the city lights below them like a code he didn't need to crack.

"Promise me something," she had said once, her voice half-asleep and serious at the same time.

"Anything."

"Promise me that no matter what happens — no matter how bad things get — you'll remember that you were loved. Completely. That someone sat here with you in the dark and thought you were the most extraordinary thing she'd ever been confused by. Promise me you'll keep that."

He had promised. He kept the promise in the only way he had left — as fuel.

He found the island on a Thursday. Exactly three weeks after she had vanished, to the day. He almost missed it — it was referenced in a chain of encoded coordinates buried in a deleted file on the private server of a minister whose corruption he had been documenting as a secondary target, and it had the particular quality of data that has been hidden by someone who knows exactly what they're hiding. It was there and not there, like a reflection in water — you had to look slightly to the side to see it clearly.

Eden. The name made his stomach turn with a specific physical violence.

He planned the insertion for five days. Every camera position. Every guard rotation. Every frequency the security system used. He built a model of the island in wire-frame on his primary screen and walked through it until he knew it in the dark, until his hands moved through the digital space of it like a space he had lived in.

On a Wednesday at 2 a.m. he took a boat.

* * *

The island at night was a particular kind of beautiful — the kind that makes you angry. Tropical, lush, the water around it lit phosphorescent where small waves broke against the rocks. From a distance it looked exactly like what it claimed to be: a paradise. This, Dom thought, grimly, with his jaw set hard, was perhaps the most honest thing about it. Paradise was always, when you got close enough, a cage with a better view.

He cut the security system from a junction point on the east side — thirty seconds of careful work, steady hands, the training and the rage kept carefully separate from each other in his mind. Then he moved.

Through the tree line. Past the first building — administrative, lights off. He remembered — unbidden, complete, vivid as the moment it happened — the first thing she had ever said to him that pierced something essential. Not the café line about hacking his drink. Not the Ferris wheel. Something earlier. The second time they had met at Caruso's, the afternoon that was the second date neither of them named. She had been looking at him across the table with the focused, slightly tilted attention she gave to things she was trying to understand, and she had said, out of nowhere: "Do you know what I think about people who live in systems? I think they forget that systems are made of choices. Every rule that exists, every structure, every wall — someone chose it. And someone can un-choose it."

He had argued with her. He had said something about institutional inertia and the limits of individual agency. She had listened patiently and then said: "You're describing why it's hard. I'm describing why it's possible. Those are different conversations."

He had thought about that conversation approximately four hundred times since she disappeared. He thought about it now, kneeling at her grave in the amber light.

She had been right about that too.

"I miss you," he said. "I'll miss you for the rest of my life. That's not — that's not a burden, I want you to know that. Missing you is one of the only things that still feels true. Everything else has changed and is changing and I don't know what comes next, but missing you feels like holding something real. So I'm keeping it."

His voice cracked on the last sentence. He let it. There was no one here to be strong for.

"I'm going to lead Silido. My grandfather has asked and I've — I've thought about it and it is, honestly, the most complicated situation I have ever been in, and you would have had approximately eleven opinions about it, all of them correct, and I would have argued with all of them and eventually, probably, done exactly what you advised." He almost smiled. "I'm going to try to do what you would have advised. I'm going to try to lead it in the direction you would have pointed."

He was quiet for a moment. The last of the sun was going from the sky, the orange giving way to the deep blue of the coming night, the first stars arriving with their indifferent brightness.

"I hope you're somewhere dancing," he said. "Loudly. With terrible music choices and no self-consciousness whatsoever. I hope wherever you are, there are strawberry candies and good books and someone who listens the way you always said I listened — like they mean it."

He placed his hand flat against the stone for a moment. The way you place your hand against something you want to remember the temperature of.

"I love you," he said. "That doesn't change. Not by any amount of time, not by anything that happens next. I love you. I will always love you. And every good thing I manage to do from here — every instance of the system being changed rather than merely moved through — that belongs to you. You started it. You, in a café, spilling my coffee. You started all of it."

He stood up. The hill was very quiet around him. The city below was beginning its evening lights — all those squares and lines and bright arterial roads, the circuit board he had shown her from the Ferris wheel, the code she had said she wanted to live inside.

He stood there until the stars were fully out. He didn't need to hurry. There was nowhere else he needed to be right now.

* * *

Zir was waiting at the cemetery gate. Beside him stood the twelve men and two women of the inner circle, all of them in black, all of them silent. As Dom walked through the gate, Zir stepped forward and they stood facing each other in the dark — the old man and the young one, the silver-haired architect and the hacker who had cracked his way to the truth through sheer will and grief and love.

Zir held his gaze for a long moment. Then he bowed his head. A small movement, simple and complete. Behind him, without instruction, the inner circle did the same.

Dom looked at his grandfather. At the face that was, he realised, the oldest mirror he had — the face that showed him, in its lines and its stillness and its complicated love, where he had come from. He had spent his adult life looking away from that. He would not look away anymore.

"I'll lead," Dom said. His voice was quiet and clear in the night air. "I'll lead. But you should know — the way I lead won't look like the way you led. I'm going to do this differently. I'm going to try to use what we have to change what shouldn't exist, not to protect what always has." He paused. "She would have said that was the only thing worth doing with any kind of power."

Zir looked at him for a long moment. And something in the old man's face — something that had been very controlled, very contained, for a very long time — moved.

"Then lead," he said simply.

Epilogue: The Code She Wrote in Him

There is a café on a narrow street in the old part of the city — not Caruso's anymore, Caruso's closed a year ago and something sleeker and louder replaced it, as always happens. But a street away, there is a small place with old wood tables and the smell of cardamom, and in the corner table with the sightline to both exits, a young man sometimes sits with a laptop and a coffee that he actually drinks while it's hot.

He has a scar on his forearm that he doesn't explain. He has the eyes of someone who has seen things that required adjusting to, and adjusted. He has a quality of attention — when he talks to someone, when he looks at someone — that people remark on afterward, without quite being able to describe what it was. Like being genuinely seen. Like being worth the effort of seeing.

Some nights he goes to the hill above the city. He brings white roses when the season has them, and other flowers when it doesn't, and he talks for a while in the dark. He tells her what has changed. What he's fighting. What the work is. He tells her when he gets it wrong, which he does, regularly, because the work is hard and he is still, underneath everything, the same man who needed to be taught that love isn't a program you can back up against loss.

He tells her when he gets it right, too. He tells her about the island — shut down completely, six months after the bunker, every structure burned and its occupants in the places people like that belong. He tells her about the other places, too, because there were other places, and he is working through them with the same methodical fury he has given everything since the night of the café and the spilled coffee.

He tells her about the people he's found — the ones who need the kind of protection that official channels won't provide, the ones the system has decided are not worth protecting. He tells her about what Silido is becoming under his direction: not cleaner, exactly — he has no illusions about what he is and what the organisation is, about the moral arithmetic of the world he operates in — but pointed differently. Aimed at something she would have recognised as worth aiming at.

The stars come out while he talks. He stays until he has said everything he came to say. Then he stands, and touches the stone, and says the same thing he always says at the end:

I love you. I'm not silent anymore.

He walks back down the hill through the amber streetlight, toward the city that is always, always, always changing — its circuits being rewired by invisible hands, its systems being quietly, persistently, unreasonably challenged by people who were told it couldn't be done and decided to try anyway.

The cold hacker and the warm girl she made of him walk together through the dark.

The world has not been fixed. The world will never be fixed, not completely, not finally — it is not the kind of problem that admits a permanent solution. But it has been changed, in specific and documented ways, and it will be changed again, and the people who depended on it staying the same have learned, at considerable cost, that dependence on a thing's permanence is not the same as that thing being permanent.

"She said: love is the only thing that hackers can't delete.

She was right. He has tried. Not to delete it — to contain it, to grieve it into something manageable, to convert it into pure function and leave the feeling behind.

It doesn't work. It never works. It is still there, in the centre of every decision he makes — the warm, inextinguishable, absolutely unreasonable fact of having been loved by Mariela Delgado.

He has stopped trying to delete it.
He has learned, slowly, to call it his greatest advantage."
— The End —
